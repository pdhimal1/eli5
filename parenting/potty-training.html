üéØ The Big Picture

Potty training is teaching your child to recognize when they need to pee or poop, and use the toilet instead of diapers.

But here's the secret: you can't force it! It's like teaching a caterpillar to fly - it happens when they're ready!

üí° The average age for potty training completion varies wildly - anywhere from 2 to 4 years old is normal! There's no "right" age.

üìÖ When Are Kids Ready?

üë∂ 0-18 months

Too young! Body can't control it yet

üßí 18-24 months

Some start showing interest

‚ú® 2-3 years

Most common time to start

üßë 3-4 years

Still normal if not done!

‚úÖ Readiness Checklist

Your child might be ready if they show MOST of these signs:

Can stay dry for 2+ hours (bladder control)
Hides or goes to a corner to poop (knows it's happening)
Shows interest in the bathroom or your toilet habits
Can pull pants up and down
Can follow simple instructions
Tells you AFTER they've gone in their diaper
Doesn't like wet/dirty diapers (wants to be changed)

Fewer checks? That's okay! Just wait a bit longer. Pushing too early often backfires.

ü™ú The Gentle Approach

Dr. Bjorkman and Dr. Aliza Pressman suggest these principles:

  • Follow their lead - wait for interest, don't force
  • No shame, no pressure - accidents are NORMAL, not failures
  • Model it - let them see you use the bathroom (weird but effective!)
  • Read books about it - normalizes the process
  • Make it accessible - potty seat in the bathroom they can sit on anytime
  • Celebrate tries, not just success - "You sat on the potty! Awesome!"

üö´ Myths vs Reality

‚ùå Myth: Earlier is Better

Starting at 18 months will make them "smarter" or more independent.

‚úÖ Reality

Kids train faster when they're truly ready. Starting too early often leads to power struggles.

‚ùå Myth: Night Training = Day Training

Once they're day trained, they should stay dry at night too.

‚úÖ Reality

Night dryness is biological, not behavioral. Many kids need night diapers until 5-7 years old!

‚ùå Myth: Rewards Work Best

Candy, stickers, and prizes will motivate them.

‚úÖ Reality

External rewards can create pressure. Internal motivation ("I did it!") works better long-term.

ü§Ø What About Regression?

Sometimes kids who were potty trained start having accidents again. This is SUPER common and usually means:

  • ü§í Big life change (new sibling, moving, starting school)
  • ü§ß Illness or constipation
  • üò∞ Stress or anxiety
  • üí§ Just needing more time for the skill to solidify
üö® Never punish accidents! It creates shame and makes things worse. Stay calm: "Oops, pee goes in the potty. Let's clean up together."

üí° Pro Tips from the Experts

  • Wait for calm times - don't start during big transitions
  • Naked time - some kids do better without the confusion of diapers/undies at first
  • Potty before leaving - make it routine, not a question ("Time to try potty" not "Do you want to try?")
  • Constipation check - if it hurts to poop, they'll hold it, making everything harder
  • Boys: start sitting - easier to learn both at once, standing can come later
